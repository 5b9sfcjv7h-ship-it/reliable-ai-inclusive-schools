# Writing Style Guide

This guide governs all visible website copy. It is permanent and overrides default writing habits.

## Positioning

AI in schools is no longer just a technology issue. It is an implementation, governance, workforce, and value-for-money issue.

Reliable AI-supported inclusive practice is an implementation capability, not a tool choice.

## Voice

Write like a public-sector advisory microsite (KPMG, Deloitte, Nous), not an academic abstract, university assignment, teacher blog, or AI-generated policy document. The voice should be:

- human
- executive
- public-sector credible
- consulting-oriented
- economically literate
- implementation-focused
- clear enough for school leaders and government readers
- still credible to academics as a secondary audience

## Avoid

- excessive em dashes
- obvious AI phrasing
- long abstract sentences
- "exploring how..." overuse
- vague transformation language
- hype about AI
- unsupported claims about cost savings
- generic academic phrasing
- "this research investigates..." unless genuinely needed

## Prefer these concepts

- implementation
- value for money
- risk control
- quality assurance
- workload reduction
- workforce capability
- governance
- service quality
- system readiness
- operational consistency
- professional judgement
- scalable practice
- measurable impact
- responsible AI use

## Rules

1. Remove most em dashes. Use periods, commas, or short clauses instead.
2. Use shorter sentences. Break long sentences into two.
3. Use active verbs. Avoid passive constructions.
4. Make the economic and implementation rationale clear in every section that can support it.
5. Keep inclusive education central to the framing.
6. Keep professional judgement central — AI supports judgement, it does not replace it.
7. Make cautious claims only. Do not overclaim savings, impact, or outcomes without evidence.
8. Prefer concrete, operational language over abstract academic framing.
